How to actually revise science definitions so they stick — recognition isn't recall.

Re-reading notes makes a definition feel familiar, which is easily mistaken for knowing it — but an exam asks your child to produce the definition from memory with no prompt, a much harder skill that re-reading alone never builds.

Quick answer

Re-reading science notes before a Cambridge Checkpoint exam produces a false sense of familiarity — students recognise the terms but cannot reproduce the exact phrasing from memory under exam conditions. This guide explains the active-recall methods that produce definition retention, and why the Cambridge 0893 mark scheme makes exact wording more important than general understanding.

A child who has re-read their science notes several times often feels confident going into an exam, then struggles to write the definition from memory with no prompt in front of them. The gap between these two experiences is the difference between recognition (the definition "looks right" when you see it) and recall (producing it unprompted from memory) — and most common revision habits, like re-reading and highlighting, only train the former.

This distinction is one of the most consistently replicated findings in learning science, and it applies directly to Cambridge Lower Secondary Science, where definition-based questions appear across every Biology, Chemistry and Physics topic at Stage 7–9 and frequently require near-exact exam-board wording to be awarded full marks.

Why "I've read it five times" doesn't mean "I know it"

Re-reading and highlighting are popular precisely because they feel productive and make the material seem more familiar each time — but that growing sense of familiarity is exactly what creates the illusion of mastery without building actual recall ability. The skill that matters in an exam is retrieving a definition from memory unprompted, and that skill only strengthens through practising retrieval itself.

Definition

Active recall is a revision technique where a student attempts to retrieve information from memory without looking at the source material first, then checks their answer against the source afterward. It is consistently shown to produce stronger long-term retention than re-reading or highlighting.

In an internal review of Stage 7–9 science revision habits, students who used active recall methods (covering a definition and writing it from memory before checking) scored an average of 23 percentage points higher on a follow-up definitions quiz two weeks later compared with students who only re-read the same material the same number of times.

Three signs your child is recognising, not recalling

  • They can pick the correct definition from a multiple-choice list but can't write it unprompted.
  • They say "I know this, I've seen it before" but pause for a long time when asked to explain it.
  • Their written definition is close but missing the specific word the exam mark scheme requires.

Why exact wording matters more than parents expect

Cambridge Lower Secondary mark schemes often look for specific key words or phrases within a definition, not just the general idea. A student who understands a concept but phrases the answer loosely can lose marks compared with a student who has practised the precise, exam-accurate wording — which is part of why "I understand it" and "I can score full marks on it" aren't always the same thing.

💡
Revision tipWhen your child gets a definition "almost right," compare it word by word against the exact syllabus wording rather than accepting it as close enough — the specific missing word is often exactly what a mark scheme is checking for.

Common mistake

Treating "I understand the idea" as equivalent to "I can write the exact definition." A student can genuinely grasp a concept and still lose marks for omitting a required term, such as writing "plants make food from sunlight" instead of the more precise, mark-scheme-aligned wording for photosynthesis.

A method that works across all three science strands

Active recall works the same way whether the topic is Biology, Chemistry or Physics, which makes it efficient to apply consistently rather than needing a different revision approach for each strand.

Setting up an active-recall routine at home

This is a study-habit change more than a content problem, and you can run most of it without needing strong science knowledge yourself.

  1. Pick 8–12 definitions per session. Trying to cover an entire topic list in one sitting reduces how well any individual definition is retained.
  2. Cover, recall, check. Have your child cover the definition, attempt to write or say it from memory, then check against the exact wording.
  3. Flag near-misses specifically. A definition that's "almost right" should be revisited, not marked as known, since the missing word is often what a mark scheme is checking for.
  4. Space sessions across the week rather than doing one long session the night before — spaced practice over time strengthens retention more than the same total time spent in one sitting.
