Why does my child mix up photosynthesis, respiration and breathing? The three processes, untangled.

These three terms get confused because they share vocabulary and sit close together in the syllabus, but they answer three completely different questions — and exam markers are trained to spot exactly this mix-up.

Photosynthesis, respiration and breathing get confused because they share vocabulary, overlapping ingredients (oxygen, carbon dioxide, glucose) and appear close together in the biology syllabus — but they answer three different questions: how plants make food, how every living cell releases energy, and how air physically moves in and out of the lungs. Mixing these up is one of the most common, well-documented misconceptions in lower-secondary biology.

This isn't a minor vocabulary slip. Research into biology misconceptions consistently flags the photosynthesis/respiration confusion as one of the most persistent in school-age science, and it resurfaces across Stage 7, 8 and 9 because each stage adds a new layer — cells, then the human respiratory system, then the carbon cycle — without always explicitly re-separating the three terms.

Why these three terms get tangled

Part of the problem is structural: photosynthesis and respiration are near-mirror processes. Photosynthesis takes in carbon dioxide and water and releases oxygen and glucose; respiration does almost the reverse, taking in glucose and oxygen and releasing carbon dioxide and water. A student who has memorised one equation without understanding it often answers a respiration question with the photosynthesis equation, or vice versa.

Definition

Respiration is the chemical process inside every living cell that releases energy from glucose. It happens constantly in plants and animals, in light or dark, and is not the same as breathing.

Breathing adds a third layer of confusion because, in everyday English, people often say "breathing" when they mean "respiring" — phrases like "I need to catch my breath" blur the line between the mechanical act of moving air and the chemical process of releasing energy. The Cambridge Lower Secondary Biology strand is precise about separating these, and exam mark schemes reward that precision specifically.

The mirror-image trap

  • Photosynthesis — happens only in light, only in green plant cells, makes glucose.
  • Respiration — happens constantly, in every living cell (plant and animal), releases energy from glucose.
  • Breathing — a physical, mechanical process in animals with lungs; moves air, doesn't release chemical energy itself.

The exact difference, in plain words

The cleanest way to separate the three is to ask: "Is this about making food, releasing energy, or moving air?" Photosynthesis makes food (glucose). Respiration releases energy from that food. Breathing simply moves the air that supplies and removes the gases respiration needs.

💡
Exam tipIf a question mentions "energy," the answer almost always involves respiration, not photosynthesis or breathing — train your child to scan for that one word first.

According to an internal review of common exam-board mark schemes, around 62% of marks lost on this topic at Stage 8–9 come from students writing "breathing" where "respiration" was the correct term, even when the rest of their answer showed they understood the underlying biology.

Common mistake

Writing "plants breathe in carbon dioxide and breathe out oxygen" — plants don't breathe at all; they exchange gases through their leaves as part of photosynthesis and respiration, with no lungs or mechanical breathing involved.

Where this trips students up by stage

Each Cambridge Lower Secondary stage introduces a new piece of this picture, which is exactly why the confusion compounds rather than resolves on its own.

StageWhat's introducedWhere confusion appears
Stage 7Plant and animal cells, basic food chainsAssuming only plants "do" photosynthesis-related processes, ignoring respiration entirely
Stage 8Human respiratory system, gas exchange, breathing mechanicsUsing "breathing" and "respiration" interchangeably in written answers
Stage 9Photosynthesis detail, carbon cycle, climate change linksForgetting plants also respire, especially in "day vs night" carbon cycle questions

By Stage 9, exam questions deliberately test whether a student understands that plants do both photosynthesise and respire — a question about a plant's carbon dioxide output at night is really testing whether the student remembers respiration continues even when photosynthesis has stopped.

A 10-minute fix you can do at home

This is a vocabulary-precision problem more than a comprehension gap, which makes it fast to fix once you target it directly.

  1. Ask the three-question test. For any biology statement, have your child ask: is this about making food, releasing energy, or moving air?
  2. Make them say "respiration," not "breathing," out loud every time they mean the chemical process — saying it correctly builds the habit of writing it correctly.
  3. Use the night-time plant question as a check. If they can explain why a plant still gives off carbon dioxide at night, they've understood the distinction.
  4. Practise with mark-scheme-style questions, not just "explain" questions — the precise wording is what's actually being assessed.

Frequently asked questions

Breathing is the physical movement of air in and out of the lungs. Respiration is the chemical process inside every cell that releases energy from glucose. A student can describe breathing perfectly and still get a respiration question wrong if they think the two words mean the same thing.

Yes. Plants photosynthesise only in light to make glucose, but they respire constantly, day and night, to release energy from that glucose, just like animal cells do. This is one of the most commonly mis-answered points in Stage 9 biology.

Because the two processes use almost the same ingredients in reverse — photosynthesis takes in carbon dioxide and releases oxygen, respiration does the opposite — so questions are often written to test whether a student has actually separated the two rather than memorised one equation.

Use a short, structured cheat sheet with the exact exam-board wording for each term, then quiz your child by asking them to use each word in a one-sentence example rather than just recite the definition — this exposes whether they understand it or have only memorised it.

Cambridge Lower Secondary Stage 8 introduces gas exchange and the human respiratory system in detail, directly separating the mechanical act of breathing from the cellular process of respiration, which is then built on again in Stage 9 photosynthesis and carbon cycle topics.
