Where to find Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint past papers (and what to do next).

The honest answer to a question thousands of Cambridge parents search for every year — what's publicly available, what isn't, and what to do once you've run out of official papers.

"Cambridge checkpoint past papers free download" is one of the most-searched queries among parents of Cambridge Lower Secondary students. The frustration behind that search is real: unlike Cambridge IGCSE, where past papers and mark schemes are released publicly on a rolling basis, the Checkpoint past paper archive works differently — and most parents only find this out after spending time searching.

This post covers exactly what's available, where to find it, how to use it well, and what to do when you've exhausted it.

Key takeaways

  • 01Cambridge distributes Checkpoint past papers to registered schools, not to the public. The public-facing site has specimen papers, not the full series archive.
  • 02Specimen papers are structured identically to the real assessment — same format, same mark allocation, same command words. They are the right thing to use for format familiarisation.
  • 03Past papers tell you what Cambridge tests. Topic-specific revision materials teach you how to answer those questions. Both are needed; one comes first.
  • 04Once you've done the available specimen papers, the highest-leverage next step is targeted topic revision on whichever strand the paper revealed as the weakest.

Why Checkpoint past papers are hard to find

Cambridge IGCSE releases past papers publicly through its Past Papers portal (usually the last two years' papers). Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint works differently: past papers and mark schemes are distributed to registered schools via the Cambridge School Support Hub, a password-protected platform for school administrators and teachers. Individual parents do not have direct access to it.

This isn't a deliberate limitation unique to Cambridge — it's the standard approach for formative Lower Secondary assessments globally, where releasing papers publicly would reduce their validity as internal school benchmarking tools. But it does mean parents hit a wall that doesn't exist for IGCSE or A-Level revision.

Cambridge School Support Hub

The platform where Cambridge distributes past papers, mark schemes and teacher resources to registered Cambridge schools. Access is for schools only. Parents can request that their child's school share specimen materials, but cannot access the Hub directly.

What is publicly available

Despite the above, there are legitimate, Cambridge-published materials you can access without school login:

Specimen assessment materials

Cambridge publishes specimen papers and mark schemes for each Lower Secondary subject on its public website. These are found on the individual subject pages for Mathematics (0862), Science (0893) and Computing (0860) under "Assessment" → "Specimen materials". Specimen papers are Cambridge-created, use the same format as the real assessment, and include mark schemes. They are not labelled as past papers but they function identically for revision purposes.

Sample assessment materials in the curriculum frameworks

The published curriculum frameworks for each subject include sample questions and mark schemes embedded within the document. These are shorter than a full paper but they are the most accurately calibrated examples of what Cambridge examiners are looking for at each stage.

Papers your school has shared

Ask your child's school whether it can share any past series papers or school-marked tests. Many Cambridge schools run internal mock assessments using previous Checkpoint papers and are willing to share them for home revision once the internal use is over.

Where to look on Cambridge's websiteGo to cambridgeinternational.org → Cambridge Lower Secondary → your subject (Mathematics/Science/Computing) → Assessment. The specimen paper download is usually listed under "Assessment materials" or "Specimen materials". It's not behind a login.

Avoid unofficial sources

Third-party sites offering "Cambridge Checkpoint past paper packs" often include unofficial papers, papers from different assessment series, or materials written for IGCSE rather than Lower Secondary. These aren't reliable for format familiarisation. Stick to materials you can verify came from Cambridge directly.

How to use past papers effectively

Once you have the specimen paper, use it as a diagnostic first, not as revision material. The goal of the first sitting isn't to get everything right — it's to find out which strand or topic has the most wrong answers.

  1. Sit it under timed conditions. No notes, no help. Full time as specified on the paper. This gives you a realistic strand-by-strand picture.
  2. Mark using the mark scheme, not intuition. Cambridge mark schemes are specific about which steps earn marks and which don't. Marking loosely inflates the apparent score and hides real gaps.
  3. Record wrong answers by strand. Which strand (Number, Algebra, Geometry, Statistics in Maths; Biology, Chemistry, Physics in Science) had the most errors? That's your revision priority.
  4. Don't redo the same paper to measure progress. Familiarity with the questions inflates the re-sit score. Save the specimen paper for format familiarisation and use different practice materials for gap-closing work.

What to do after you run out of papers

The specimen paper is one document. Once you've used it for diagnosis, you need a different type of resource to build the skill. This is where topic-specific practice comes in.

If the specimen paper showed your child losing marks on the Algebra strand, doing more mixed-topic practice papers won't fix the Algebra gap efficiently — it will give you more data confirming the gap exists. A single-topic Algebra booster, working through Foundation to Stretch difficulty questions with full worked solutions, is the mechanism that closes it.

Frequently asked questions

Cambridge distributes Checkpoint past papers and mark schemes to registered schools via the Cambridge School Support Hub, not to the general public. What is publicly available includes specimen papers on the Cambridge International website, and some older series papers that Cambridge has released. Individual parents cannot directly access the full series past paper archive without going through a registered school.

Cambridge publishes specimen assessment materials on its public website for each subject. For Maths (0862), Science (0893) and Computing (0860), the specimen papers and mark schemes are available from the Cambridge International Lower Secondary pages. These are the official sample papers Cambridge provides to show the format and difficulty of the assessment. They are structured identically to real past papers.

Past papers tell you what Cambridge tests; topic-specific revision materials teach you how to answer those questions. Once you've exhausted the available specimen and past papers, the most productive next step is targeted topic work: identify which strand or topic had the most wrong answers in the past paper, and work through a dedicated single-topic resource for that strand. This converts the diagnosis a past paper provides into the skill-building that moves the score.

Not as a primary resource. IGCSE (e.g. Cambridge IGCSE Mathematics 0580) is a higher level than Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint, so Stage 7–9 students doing IGCSE papers will find most of the content either unfamiliar or beyond their current stage. The exception is the very easiest IGCSE questions, which can give timed-practice experience, but they shouldn't be used as a guide to what the Checkpoint actually assesses.
