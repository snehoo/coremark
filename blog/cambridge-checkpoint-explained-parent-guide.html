Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint explained: a parent's guide.

What Cambridge Checkpoint actually is, whether your child has to sit it, how the 0.0–6.0 score works, and the most efficient way to help with revision once you know the result.

If your child's school has mentioned "Checkpoint" and you're not entirely sure what that means for your Stage 7, 8 or 9 student, you are far from alone. Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint is one of the least-explained parts of the Cambridge International pathway, mostly because schools handle the logistics and rarely send parents a plain-English summary.

This guide covers what it is, whether your child actually has to sit it, how the scoring works, and what to do with the result once it arrives.

Key takeaways

  • 01Checkpoint is a diagnostic test in English, Mathematics and Science for Stage 7 to 9 learners — not a pass or fail qualification.
  • 02It is optional. Cambridge International leaves the decision to enter learners to the individual school.
  • 03Each subject has two test papers, with results reported on a 0.0 to 6.0 scale, overall and per strand.
  • 04Computing is part of the curriculum but is not one of the three subjects assessed by a Checkpoint test.

What is Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint, exactly?

Cambridge Lower Secondary is the curriculum stage run by Cambridge International for learners roughly aged 11 to 14, covering Stage 7, Stage 8 and Stage 9. Checkpoint sits at the end of that programme as a formal assessment tool. According to Cambridge International's own Checkpoint page, the tests are designed to give detailed feedback on a learner's strengths and weaknesses across English, Mathematics and Science.

Definition

Cambridge Checkpoint is not the same kind of assessment as Cambridge IGCSE or O Level. There is no overall pass mark and no certificate that universities or employers look for. It exists purely to tell the school, the teacher and the family where a learner currently stands, topic by topic, before they move further into the Cambridge pathway.

Is it compulsory, and how does scoring work?

No, Checkpoint is not compulsory. The decision to enter learners for a given test series sits with the school, not with Cambridge International. Many Cambridge schools choose to run Checkpoint every year because the feedback is useful for tracking progress and for early familiarisation with formal exam conditions, but a school can decide not to enter a particular cohort.

Each subject is assessed through two question papers, sat under exam-style conditions at school. After marking, Cambridge produces a result reported on a scale from 0.0 to 6.0 — both for the subject as a whole and, more usefully, for each individual strand within that subject.

💡
What actually mattersBecause the test is diagnostic rather than pass or fail, there is no single number that counts as "passing." What matters for revision planning is the pattern across strands — a learner might score comfortably in Number but noticeably lower in Geometry, and that gap is the part worth addressing.

Stage 7, 8 and 9 — what actually changes

The three Lower Secondary stages build on each other rather than restarting each year. Broadly, this is how demand increases across Mathematics, Science and Computing, based on Cambridge's published curriculum frameworks:

StageTypical ageWhat tends to step up
Stage 7~11–12Foundational strand concepts: integers and place value, basic algebraic expressions, cells and simple chemistry, introductory flowcharts and text-based programming.
Stage 8~12–13Concepts deepen and combine: factorising and solving equations, pseudocode and searching algorithms, atomic structure, ecosystems and chemical reactions.
Stage 9~13–14Direct preparation for IGCSE-style thinking: simultaneous equations, iteration and algorithm comparison, density, energy transfer and reactivity series.

For the full topic list inside each subject, Cambridge publishes the official curriculum frameworks for Lower Secondary Mathematics (0862), Science (0893) and Computing (0860).

The strand breakdown is the part worth acting on — it turns "needs to do better in Maths" into a specific, fixable topic.

How to help your child prepare (without becoming the teacher)

Most parents helping with Cambridge Checkpoint revision are not subject specialists, and that's fine. A few practical habits make the biggest difference regardless of subject knowledge:

  • Start from the actual gap, not a random past paper. Use the strand breakdown from the last report, or the topic your child's teacher has flagged, to decide what to revise first.
  • Revise one topic at a time. A 60-question mixed workbook tests everything at once and makes it hard to tell whether a wrong answer was a topic gap or a careless mistake.
  • Get familiar with Cambridge's command words. A surprising number of marks are lost on misreading the instruction rather than not knowing the content — Cambridge publishes a guide to understanding command words.
  • Use materials that show full worked solutions, not just final answers. Method marks matter in Cambridge marking.
  • If you're not confident in the subject yourself, use a resource written for non-expert parents. A short guide explaining what to look for in your child's working closes most of the gap.

Common mistake

Treating the headline Checkpoint score as the full picture and skipping the strand-level breakdown. The breakdown converts a vague "needs to do better in Maths" into a specific, fixable topic instruction.
