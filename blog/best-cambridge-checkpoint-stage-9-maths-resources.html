Quick answer

Stage 9 is the Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint year, and the Algebra and Geometry strands introduce topics that do not appear at earlier stages — so resources written for Stage 7 or 8 miss the new content entirely. This guide compares revision resources specifically for the Stage 9 Checkpoint and explains how to use strand scores (0.0–6.0) to direct revision.

By Stage 9, the stakes are different. The Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint is at the end of this year, and the Algebra and Geometry strands in particular introduce topics that don't appear at all in Stage 8 — simultaneous equations, Pythagoras' theorem, trigonometric ratios, combined probability. At this point, a "general revision" workbook is less likely to cover the right ground than a targeted resource built specifically for the Cambridge 0862 strand structure.

Here's a practical comparison of what's available, and what to prioritise when revision time is limited.

What the Stage 9 Checkpoint actually tests

Cambridge publishes the full Lower Secondary Mathematics (0862) curriculum framework. At Stage 9, the four strands cover:

  • Number: standard form, powers and roots (including fractional indices), recurring decimals, compound percentage change, reverse percentage, ratio and proportion at higher complexity.
  • Algebra: expanding and factorising quadratics, simultaneous equations, linear and quadratic inequalities, nth term of quadratic sequences, plotting and interpreting quadratic and other non-linear graphs.
  • Geometry and Measure: Pythagoras' theorem in 2D and 3D, trigonometric ratios (sin, cos, tan), arc length and sector area, volume of prisms and pyramids, combined and successive transformations.
  • Statistics and Probability: cumulative frequency and box plots, histograms, combined and conditional probability, two-way tables for independent events.

Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint — Stage 9

The end-of-Lower-Secondary assessment, usually sat at age 13–14. Cambridge reports results on a 0.0 to 6.0 scale, overall and per strand. The strand breakdown is the revision planning tool; the overall score is the headline number. Many schools use Checkpoint results to inform subject setting for IGCSE or equivalent.

Stage 9 trap

Using Stage 8 revision materials for Stage 9 prep. Stage 8 materials won't include Pythagoras, trigonometry, simultaneous equations or cumulative frequency — all of which appear in the Stage 9 Checkpoint. Check explicitly that any resource you buy covers Stage 9 topics, not just "Lower Secondary" in general.

Frequently asked questions

The Cambridge Checkpoint Maths assessment (0862) at Stage 9 draws from four strands that have built up across Stages 7 to 9: Number (powers and roots, fractions, decimals, percentages, standard form, ratio), Algebra (expressions, equations, simultaneous equations, inequalities, sequences, quadratic and linear graphs), Geometry and Measure (Pythagoras' theorem, trigonometry, advanced area and volume, transformations), and Statistics and Probability (two-way tables, scatter graphs, combined probability). The Checkpoint is the summative assessment of all three Lower Secondary stages.

The Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint is typically sat at the end of Stage 9 (usually age 13–14, the third year of Lower Secondary). Schools register for May or October series. Check with your child's school for the exact sitting date, as it varies by school and country.

Cambridge reports Checkpoint results on a 0.0 to 6.0 scale, both for the overall score and for each of the four strands (Number, Algebra, Geometry and Measure, Statistics and Probability). The strand breakdown is more useful for revision planning than the overall score, because it pinpoints exactly which area of the curriculum needs targeted work.

Stage 9 introduces several genuinely new concepts that don't appear in Stage 8 — most notably simultaneous equations, Pythagoras' theorem, trigonometric ratios, standard form, and combined probability. It also extends Stage 8 topics (quadratic graphs, compound interest, more complex transformations) to a higher level of demand. Students who have solid Stage 7 and 8 foundations typically find Stage 9 manageable; those with gaps in earlier stages often find Stage 9 topics harder than they need to be.
