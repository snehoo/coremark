What is the Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint exam? A plain-English answer for parents

Checkpoint appears on school calendars and parent communication without much explanation. This guide covers what it actually is, what it tests, how it's scored, and what schools use the results for.

Quick answer

It covers Maths, Science, and English, reports on a 0–6 scale, and is not a formal qualification. Schools use results to identify gaps before IGCSE begins.

The Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint is an assessment designed and administered by Cambridge Assessment International Education (CAIE). It sits at the end of the Cambridge Lower Secondary programme — typically taken by students at the end of Stage 9, which corresponds to roughly Year 9, Class 9, or age 13–14, depending on the school.

The most important thing to understand about Checkpoint is what it is not: it is not a formal qualification. Unlike IGCSE, which awards a graded certificate that appears on university and employment records, Checkpoint produces a diagnostic report. There is no pass or fail. No certificate is issued that students present for future admissions.

Definition

Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint is an optional external assessment at the end of the Cambridge Lower Secondary programme (Stage 9). It reports student performance on a standardised 0.0–6.0 scale per subject, broken down by curriculum strand. It is a diagnostic tool, not a formal qualification.

What the Checkpoint is — and what it isn't

Cambridge Lower Secondary is a two-part curriculum framework: the programme itself (Stage 7 to Stage 9, covering the teaching and learning sequence) and the optional Checkpoint assessment at the end of Stage 9. The programme is compulsory in schools that use the Cambridge Lower Secondary framework; the Checkpoint exam is not.

Many parents hear "Cambridge exam" and assume it functions like IGCSE or A Level — a high-stakes test with formal grades. Checkpoint is different. Its purpose is explicitly diagnostic: to tell the student, parents, and school where performance sits relative to expected Stage 9 attainment levels, so that any gaps can be addressed before IGCSE coursework begins in Stage 10.

Common confusion

Checkpoint is not an IGCSE and does not produce an IGCSE-style grade. A student who scores 5.5 in Checkpoint Maths has not "passed Maths at IGCSE level." They have demonstrated strong Stage 9 attainment — a different thing. See our guide on Cambridge Lower Secondary vs IGCSE for the full comparison.

Which subjects are tested

Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint covers three subjects:

  • Mathematics (Cambridge syllabus code 0862) — Number, Algebra, Geometry and Measure, Statistics and Probability.
  • Science (Cambridge syllabus code 0893) — Biology, Chemistry, Physics, and Scientific Enquiry.
  • English (Cambridge syllabus code 0861) — Reading and Writing. Schools using English as a Second Language may offer code 0876 instead.

Each subject is assessed and reported independently. A student receives a separate 0–6 score for Maths, Science, and English. The three scores do not combine into a single overall number.

Paper format and timing

Each subject is assessed over two papers, each approximately 60 minutes long. The papers are written by Cambridge and marked externally by Cambridge-trained examiners — unlike regular school tests, which teachers mark themselves.

Papers are administered at the school during a set Cambridge examination window, typically in April–May for the end-of-year sitting. Results are released by Cambridge a few weeks later and shared with the school and parents in the form of a detailed strand-level report.

How the 0–6 score works

Cambridge reports Checkpoint results on a 0.0 to 6.0 standardised scale per subject, broken down further into individual curriculum strands. For Mathematics, this means a strand score for Number, Algebra, Geometry and Measure, and Statistics separately — not just one overall Maths number.

The scale is standardised, not a simple percentage. A score of 4.0 does not mean "4 out of 6 questions answered correctly." It means the student's performance places them at a specific point on a scale that has been statistically adjusted to be consistent across different exam papers and different years. For more on how the scale works, see our detailed guide on Cambridge Checkpoint score explained.

💡
The strand breakdown is more useful than the overall scoreTwo students with the same overall Maths score can have completely opposite patterns of strength and weakness across Number, Algebra, Geometry, and Statistics. Read the strand breakdown, not just the headline number, to know what to revise.

How schools use the results

Because Checkpoint is a diagnostic tool rather than a pass/fail exam, schools use the results in different ways depending on their own academic planning policies. The most common uses are:

  • Setting and grouping for IGCSE — some schools use Checkpoint strand scores to place students into core or extended tier groups for IGCSE Maths and Science.
  • Identifying support needs — students with significantly lower strand scores in one area may be offered additional sessions or targeted support before IGCSE begins.
  • Recognising high attainment — strong Checkpoint scores (typically 5.0+) may be used as a signal for extension opportunities or enrichment programmes in Stage 10.
  • Parent communication — Checkpoint reports are shared with parents as a diagnostic snapshot, giving families a clear view of exactly which strand-level areas need attention at home before the IGCSE preparation phase begins.

It is always worth asking your child's school directly how they use Checkpoint results — the policies vary, and what matters practically for your child depends on the school's internal processes.

Frequently asked questions

The Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint is an optional external assessment administered by Cambridge Assessment International Education (CAIE) at the end of Stage 9, typically when students are around 14 years old. It covers Mathematics, Science, and English, and reports results on a 0.0 to 6.0 scale per subject. Checkpoint is a diagnostic tool, not a formal qualification — it does not award a certificate the way IGCSE does.

Checkpoint is optional for schools — CAIE does not require schools in the Cambridge Lower Secondary programme to enter students for Checkpoint. Many Cambridge-affiliated schools choose to enter their Stage 9 students as a transition diagnostic before IGCSE, but some schools use internal assessments instead. Whether your child takes Checkpoint depends on the school's choice.

Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint covers three subjects: Mathematics (syllabus code 0862), English (syllabus code 0861), and Science (syllabus code 0893). Some schools may also offer English as a Second Language. Each subject is assessed separately and produces an independent score on the 0 to 6 scale.

Each Checkpoint subject is assessed over two papers, each approximately 60 minutes long. For Mathematics, Paper 1 is non-calculator and Paper 2 allows a calculator. Science has a theory paper and a scientific enquiry paper. English has reading and writing papers. Papers are marked externally by Cambridge.

Checkpoint and IGCSE use completely different reporting scales and serve different purposes, so direct comparison is not meaningful. Checkpoint reports a 0.0 to 6.0 scale as a diagnostic indicator. IGCSE reports grades from A* to G (or 9 to 1 in numerically-graded syllabuses). A strong Checkpoint score (5.0+) is generally associated with readiness for Extended-tier IGCSE content, but the two scales are independent.
