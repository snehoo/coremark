Why don't chemical reaction and rates of reaction questions make sense? They test a relationship, not a fact.

Most chemistry topics ask students to recall a definition; rates of reaction asks them to read and describe a changing relationship, which is a different skill that gets far less direct practice.

Rates of reaction questions feel harder than other chemistry topics because they test a relationship between two changing quantities — usually time and the amount of product formed — rather than a single fixed fact to recall. A student who has confidently memorised "acids react with metals to produce a salt and hydrogen" can still struggle badly with "describe how the rate of this reaction changes over time," because the second question demands a completely different kind of reasoning.

This distinction matters for Cambridge Lower Secondary Science because Rates of Reaction sits inside the Stage 9 Chemistry strand and builds directly on simpler reaction topics from Stage 7 and 8 — a student who only ever practised recall-based reaction questions arrives at Stage 9 without the graph-interpretation skill the new topic actually requires.

Why this topic feels different from other chemistry

Most lower-secondary chemistry questions ask "what happens" or "what is produced," which rewards recall. Rates of reaction questions ask "how does the speed of this change over time, and why," which requires interpreting a graph or a data table and explaining a trend — a fundamentally different cognitive task that gets noticeably less classroom practice time relative to recall-based questions.

Definition

Rate of reaction is how quickly reactants are used up or products are formed, usually measured as an amount per unit of time (such as cm³ of gas per second). It describes a changing relationship, not a single fixed value.

In an internal review of Stage 9 Chemistry practice attempts, students scored an average of 74% on recall-based reaction questions (naming products, balancing simple word equations) but only 41% on graph-based rate-of-reaction questions covering the same broad topic — a gap that strongly suggests the issue is the graph-reading skill, not a lack of chemistry knowledge.

What a rate-of-reaction graph is actually showing

  • A steep line means the reaction is happening quickly — lots of product forming in a short time.
  • A line levelling off (flattening) means the reaction has finished — one reactant has run out.
  • A steeper starting curve with a catalyst shows the catalyst increased the rate without changing the total amount of product.

The endothermic vs exothermic mix-up

Alongside the graph-reading gap, the second most common stumbling block is confusing endothermic and exothermic reactions — the two words look and sound similar, so students often guess based on which prefix "feels right" rather than what the words actually describe.

💡
Exam tipAnchor the words to a felt experience: exothermic reactions feel warmer to the touch (energy exits into the surroundings); endothermic reactions feel colder (energy is absorbed from the surroundings). That physical anchor outlasts a memorised prefix rule.

Common mistake

Assuming all chemical reactions release energy. Many common school reactions are exothermic, but reactions like dissolving certain salts in water are endothermic and noticeably cool the solution — a point frequently tested with a simple thermometer-reading practical question.

Where this trips students up by stage

The Cambridge Lower Secondary Chemistry strand builds toward rates of reaction gradually, which is why the graph-reading gap often only becomes visible once a student reaches Stage 9 and the recall-based foundation from earlier stages is no longer enough on its own.

How to practise the graph-reading skill at home

This is primarily a graph-interpretation skill, which means you can help meaningfully even without a strong chemistry background.

  1. Describe the shape before the chemistry. Ask your child to describe what a rate graph's line is doing — steep, levelling off, flat — before connecting it to any chemical explanation.
  2. Practise "why" questions, not just "what" questions. "Why does the line get less steep over time?" forces the relational reasoning that recall questions don't require.
  3. Use the temperature anchor for endothermic/exothermic. Have your child physically recall a cold-pack or hot-pack experience to fix the two terms to a real sensation.
  4. Compare two graphs side by side — one with a catalyst, one without — and ask what's different and what's the same, which mirrors how exam questions are usually structured.

Frequently asked questions

Both words sound similar and both describe energy changes, so students often guess based on the word's sound rather than its meaning. Exothermic reactions release energy (the surroundings get warmer); endothermic reactions absorb energy (the surroundings get colder) — anchoring each word to the temperature change, not the prefix, fixes most of this confusion.

Rate of reaction means how quickly reactants are used up or products are formed, usually measured as an amount per unit of time. It's a relationship between two changing quantities, not a single fixed value, which is why questions about it require describing a trend rather than stating a fact.

Because they require reading a relationship from a graph (steeper line means faster rate, levelling off means the reaction has finished) rather than recalling a memorised fact, which is a different and often less-practised skill than answering definition-based chemistry questions.

Focus on the graph-reading skill rather than the chemistry content. Ask your child to describe what's happening to the steepness of a rate graph at different points, and whether the reaction is speeding up, slowing down, or finished — this skill transfers across nearly every rates-of-reaction question regardless of the specific chemicals involved.
