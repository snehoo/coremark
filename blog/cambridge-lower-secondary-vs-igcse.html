Cambridge Lower Secondary vs IGCSE: what's the difference?

These two programmes are often confused or discussed as though they are alternatives. They are not. They are sequential stages of the same educational pathway, and understanding the distinction changes how you prepare for each one.

Short answer

Cambridge Lower Secondary (Stages 7, 8, 9) is a curriculum framework for ages 11–14 that builds foundational knowledge. It is not a formal qualification. IGCSE (Stages 10 and 11) is an internationally recognised qualification, the first in the Cambridge pathway, taken at around age 16. The two are sequential: Lower Secondary prepares students for IGCSE, not an alternative to it.

The short answer

Cambridge Lower Secondary and IGCSE are two stages in a single connected educational pathway, not competing options. Cambridge Lower Secondary covers ages 11–14 (Stages 7 to 9). IGCSE follows immediately after, covering ages 14–16 (Stages 10 and 11). A student moves through Lower Secondary first, then into IGCSE.

The reason this confusion is so common is that both carry the Cambridge name and both are used in international schools worldwide. But they are fundamentally different in what they produce: Lower Secondary is a teaching programme with an optional diagnostic assessment at the end. IGCSE is a formal qualification that appears on transcripts and is used for university admissions.

What Cambridge Lower Secondary is

Cambridge Lower Secondary is a curriculum framework designed and published by Cambridge Assessment International Education (CAIE). It covers three years: Stage 7, Stage 8, and Stage 9. Students typically enter Stage 7 at around age 11 and complete Stage 9 at around age 14.

The programme defines what students learn in Mathematics (code 0862), Science (code 0893), English (code 0861), Computing (code 0860), and other subjects. It provides a structured sequence of content that builds year on year, ensuring Stage 8 content assumes Stage 7 knowledge, and Stage 9 content assumes Stage 8 knowledge.

Cambridge Lower Secondary

A three-year curriculum programme (Stages 7, 8, 9) for students aged approximately 11–14. It is a teaching framework, not a qualification. The optional external assessment at the end (Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint) produces a diagnostic score, not a certificate or formal grade.

At the end of Stage 9, schools can choose to enter students for the Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint — an external exam that reports performance on a 0–6 scale per subject. This is diagnostic: it tells the student, the parent, and the school where performance sits relative to expected Stage 9 attainment. For more on Checkpoint specifically, see our guide on what the Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint is.

What IGCSE is

The International General Certificate of Secondary Education (IGCSE) is a formal qualification administered by CAIE. Students take IGCSE examinations at the end of Stage 11 (Year 11), typically at age 15–16. The programme runs across two years: Stage 10 and Stage 11.

Unlike Cambridge Lower Secondary, IGCSE produces a graded certificate. Students receive grades on an A*–G scale (or a 9–1 scale for certain syllabuses) that are internationally recognised by universities, schools, and employers. IGCSE results appear on academic transcripts and are used in applications to universities, sixth forms, colleges, and further education programmes worldwide.

IGCSE

International General Certificate of Secondary Education — a formal qualification awarded by Cambridge Assessment International Education (CAIE) at the end of Stage 11 (age 15–16). Grades run A* to G (or 9 to 1 for numerically-graded syllabuses). Internationally recognised for university admissions.

How Lower Secondary and IGCSE connect

The two programmes are designed to connect directly. Cambridge Lower Secondary intentionally builds the foundational knowledge and skills that IGCSE then extends and examines at a higher level. The content in Stage 9 Maths, for example, directly precedes the content in IGCSE Maths Stage 10 — it is not repeated, it is assumed.

This means that gaps in Cambridge Lower Secondary understanding do not disappear when IGCSE begins. A student who reaches Stage 10 with weak Stage 8 Algebra knowledge will encounter that gap in IGCSE Algebra content that assumes Stage 8 has been mastered. The Checkpoint exam at Stage 9 exists precisely to surface these gaps before the IGCSE programme starts.

Lower Secondary is the runway. IGCSE is the takeoff. You can't skip the runway and expect a clean lift.

The transition point: end of Stage 9

The boundary between Cambridge Lower Secondary and IGCSE is the transition from Stage 9 to Stage 10. In practice, this often corresponds to an academic year change, and it is the moment when:

  • Subject choice becomes relevant for the first time. Students select which IGCSE subjects they will take, and the selection shapes their Stage 10 timetable.
  • Setting and grouping for IGCSE may happen (schools vary on this, some using Checkpoint results to inform placement).
  • The difficulty level increases noticeably. IGCSE content is more demanding than Stage 9 in both depth and examination technique.

What this means for how you prepare

The sequential nature of the two programmes has a practical implication: gaps in Lower Secondary content do not self-correct when IGCSE begins. If a student is weak in Stage 8 Algebra or Stage 9 Geometry, IGCSE Maths will immediately require those concepts as prerequisites. Revision work done during Stage 7–9 is not wasted — it is the foundation on which IGCSE marks are built.

💡
Use the Checkpoint strand breakdown, not just the scoreWhen your child's Checkpoint report arrives, look at the per-strand scores (Number, Algebra, Geometry, Statistics for Maths). These strand scores tell you specifically which Stage 7–9 topics to consolidate before IGCSE content builds on them.

For families whose children are currently in Stages 7, 8, or 9, the focus should be on mastering the Lower Secondary curriculum topic by topic — not skipping to IGCSE-level revision. The IGCSE exam is still 2–4 years away; the right preparation now is solid Lower Secondary topic fluency.
