Cambridge Checkpoint past papers vs topic boosters: which helps more?

Past papers are harder to get than most parents realise, and Cambridge itself cautions against over-relying on them. Here's what actually works, and when.

The most common revision instinct for Cambridge exams is to find past papers and work through them. For Cambridge IGCSE that instinct is reasonable — past papers go back years and are freely downloadable. For Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint, it runs into two problems: the papers are difficult to access at home, and Cambridge itself advises that heavy past-paper practice is not really what Checkpoint is designed for.

Key takeaways

  • 01Cambridge makes only one set of specimen papers publicly available. Full past papers are on a teachers-only School Support Hub.
  • 02Cambridge explicitly cautions that Checkpoint usefulness "may be reduced if extensive revision and practising of past papers is carried out."
  • 03Past papers are best used for timed, exam-condition practice once topic gaps are already closed — not for identifying or fixing those gaps.
  • 04Topic-specific practice closes individual gaps faster and gives parents something actionable to do between now and the test.
  • 05The two approaches are most powerful in sequence: topic revision first, timed past-paper practice second.

Why Checkpoint past papers are hard to find

This surprises many families because Cambridge IGCSE past papers are so easy to download. The situation for Lower Secondary Checkpoint is deliberately different. According to Cambridge's own support guidance, one set of specimen papers is available publicly, while additional series past papers are housed on the Cambridge Lower Secondary School Support Hub, which requires a registered school login. Teachers can download them; parents sitting at home typically cannot.

What Cambridge actually says

"We have always taken the view that Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint should not disrupt the normal teaching programme. Therefore, please do take into consideration that usefulness may be reduced if extensive revision and practising of past papers is carried out."

That is Cambridge telling schools, and by extension families, that Checkpoint is primarily a diagnostic snapshot, not a high-stakes exam to be crammed for using its own past papers. Which makes the question worth asking: if not past papers, then what?

What past papers are actually good for

Past papers aren't useless — they're just being used in the wrong order by most families. The genuine value of a Checkpoint-style paper is timed exam-condition practice, familiarisation with question style, and confirming readiness once topics are already secure.

✅
Past papers work well whenA learner has already worked through their weak topics and needs a full timed run-through to check readiness. Best used in the 1–2 weeks before the test itself.

Past papers don't work well when

A learner has a known gap in a specific topic. A mixed 50-question paper tells them that something went wrong, not exactly what, and not how to fix it. It can also demoralise a learner who is being exposed to topics they haven't revised yet.

Understanding Cambridge's mark scheme: M, A and B marks

One reason parents find it hard to help with Cambridge Checkpoint preparation is that the mark schemes look unfamiliar. Having even a basic understanding of how marks work helps when reviewing your child's practice.

CodeNameWhat it means
M1Method markAwarded for a correct working step, even when the final answer is wrong. This is the mark that "show your working" is designed to protect.
A1Accuracy markAwarded for the correct final answer. Usually only given when the method is also correct.
B1Independent markAwarded for a specific correct statement, regardless of whether surrounding working is right or wrong.

Definition

Error Carried Forward (ECF or FT) means that if a learner makes a mistake in one step but continues with correct method in subsequent steps, they can still earn marks for the later work. A child who shows full working and makes a single arithmetic slip is in a far better position than one who writes only a final answer that happens to be wrong.

What topic-specific practice does that past papers cannot

A topic booster — built around one strand of the curriculum — solves three problems that a mixed past paper cannot:

What a parent needs to knowPast paperTopic booster
Which specific topic is weak?Partially — a wrong answer somewhere in Number, but it could be any sub-topicYes — every question is on the same topic, so a pattern of errors is immediately visible
Why is the answer wrong?Rarely — mark schemes show the right answer but rarely explain the reasoningYes — worked solutions explain each step and flag the most common mistake
Can I help without being a subject expert?Difficult — raw mark schemes are written for teachersYes — a parent guide explains what to look for in the working
Is difficulty calibrated?No — a Checkpoint paper mixes Foundation, Standard and Stretch across many topicsYes — Foundation, Standard and Stretch are clearly labelled within one topic

The sequence that works

The most effective preparation for Cambridge Lower Secondary Checkpoint combines both approaches, in the right order:

  1. Identify the gap first. Use the strand-level breakdown from the last Checkpoint or school report to find which topic or topics are noticeably weaker.
  2. Do topic-by-topic revision for each gap. One topic at a time, with a cheat sheet, graduated questions and full worked solutions.
  3. Do one timed full-paper practice in the final week or two. By this point, the topic gaps are closed and a full paper serves as confirmation rather than diagnosis.
"Past papers tell you that something is wrong. Topic-specific practice tells you exactly what — and shows you how to fix it before the test." CoreMark Editorial

Frequently asked questions

Additional past papers for each series are published on the Cambridge Lower Secondary School Support Hub, which requires a school login — so they're accessible to teachers, not directly to families at home. Cambridge explicitly notes that Checkpoint 'should not disrupt the normal teaching programme' and that usefulness 'may be reduced if extensive revision and practising of past papers is carried out.'

No. Cambridge IGCSE past papers are published publicly for download and widely available. Cambridge Checkpoint past papers are different — they are primarily distributed through the School Support Hub for registered Cambridge schools, and Cambridge makes only one set of specimen papers publicly accessible.
